Why native plants matter

We are in the midst of a quiet crisis. Across North America, populations of bees, butterflies, moths, beetles, and hummingbirds—the creatures responsible for pollinating roughly one-third of everything we eat—have declined sharply over the past half-century. Habitat loss is the primary driver: as meadows give way to lawns and roadsides are mowed to the edge, the web of relationships between native plants and native pollinators frays thread by thread.

Native plants are not merely decorative. Over millions of years, they have co-evolved with local insects, developing the precise flower shapes, bloom times, nectars, and chemical signals that specialist pollinators depend on. A non-native ornamental may look similar to a human eye, but offer nothing to a native bee whose life cycle is tuned to something specific. The monarch butterfly can complete its life cycle only on milkweed. The rusty-patched bumblebee requires a succession of blooms from early spring through late autumn to sustain a colony. This specificity is not fragility—it is the architecture of an ecosystem.

“A single mature native oak supports over 500 species of caterpillars. A Bradford pear—the tree planted on millions of suburban streets—supports fewer than five.”

A backyard planting of native perennials, shrubs, and trees is not a small gesture. Research from the Tallamy lab at the University of Delaware has shown that even modest patches of native habitat—as little as one-tenth of an acre—meaningfully support breeding bird populations when connected across a neighbourhood. The cumulative effect of many small gardens is measurable at the landscape scale.
